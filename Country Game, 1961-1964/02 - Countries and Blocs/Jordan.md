**Jordan** is annexed by the [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]] during [[Operation Fades Crossing]] (1961) — a LETF-only operation securing Amman, Irbid, Aqaba, and Zarqa. Reorganized as the **[[Jordanian Province of the Fade]]**, Jordan serves as the critical land bridge connecting the LETF's Levantine holdings (Israel, Lebanon, Syria) to Iraq and the Arabian Peninsula. Its annexation closes the gap in LETF's Middle Eastern territorial corridor and enables future operational expansion eastward and southward.

## Background

Jordan's geographic position makes its annexation nearly inevitable once the LETF secures Israel, Lebanon, and Syria. As the land connector between the Levant and Iraq, Jordan's status as an uncontrolled territory would create a fragmented LETF administrative map — separating the Mediterranean-coast provinces from the Mesopotamian holding. Securing Jordan eliminates this geographic fragmentation and establishes unbroken LETF territorial continuity from Egypt northward through the Levant and eastward to Iraq.

Aqaba specifically — Jordan's Red Sea port — also gives the LETF access to the Red Sea corridor, complementing its Persian Gulf access via Basra.

## Operation Fades Crossing

[[Operation Fades Crossing]] (1961) is a LETF-only operation — one of the rare mid-1961 campaigns not requiring U.S. or Swedish co-participation. Jordan's military capacity is insufficient to resist LETF forces operating from Israeli and Syrian staging positions:

- **Amman**: Jordan's capital is surrounded from north (from Syrian territory), west (from Israeli territory), and south; ground forces seize the city after modest resistance
- **Irbid**: Northern Jordan's major city, bordering Syria, is secured in a rapid northern ground advance
- **Aqaba**: Jordan's Red Sea port is seized in a separate southern operation; the port infrastructure is captured intact and converted to LETF logistical use
- **Zarqa**: Jordan's second-largest city and major military base is secured; its military infrastructure is absorbed into LETF's regional garrison network

## Jordanian Province of the Fade

The [[Jordanian Province of the Fade]] is administered as part of the LETF's broader Levantine administrative cluster alongside the Levantine Province (Lebanon) and Syrian Province. Its governance functions primarily as a logistics and transit administration — coordinating military movement along the Levant-Iraq corridor and managing Aqaba's port operations for Red Sea access.

## Strategic Consequences

Jordan's annexation has several cascading strategic effects:

- **Continental connectivity**: Completes the LETF's unbroken land route from Egypt through the Levant to Iraq, enabling efficient overland military logistics
- **Red Sea access**: Aqaba's port opens the Red Sea as a LETF naval operational zone, providing an alternative maritime route to the Persian Gulf and Indian Ocean
- **Saudi Arabia corridor**: The southern route through Aqaba and the Jordan-Saudi border becomes the primary LETF approach vector for future operations against Saudi Arabia ([[Operation Total Fade]])
- **Resistance isolation**: Jordan's annexation eliminates a potential resistance sanctuary between Israel and Iraq; displaced resistance leaders from other Levantine territories have fewer options for cross-border movement

## Legacy

Jordan's conquest is unremarkable by campaign standards — a relatively swift annexation of a smaller state that was strategically necessary for LETF territorial coherence. Its significance lies not in the campaign's drama but in its strategic consequences: the Jordanian land bridge makes the LETF's entire eastern Mediterranean-to-Gulf operational corridor possible, and Aqaba provides Red Sea access that feeds the empire's maritime reach in subsequent expansion phases.
