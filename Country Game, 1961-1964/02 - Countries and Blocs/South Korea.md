**South Korea** occupies a dual role in the campaign timeline: it serves as one of the staging bases for [[Operation Silent Halo]] — the U.S. covert operation supporting Chinese resistance against LETF occupation — before being targeted in the [[Multi-Target Nuclear Campaign]] (1962) and subsequently annexed as the **[[Korean Fade Province]]** during [[Operation Total Fade]]. South Korea's liberation with U.S. support during the collapse phase makes it one of the East Asian territories where U.S. strategic investment — first in using Korea as a covert operations base, then in supporting its resistance — produces a direct liberation outcome.

## Background

South Korea in the early campaign timeline is a U.S.-aligned East Asian state with significant military capacity and a position on the Korean Peninsula adjacent to LETF-controlled territories in North Asia and the expanding LETF Pacific network (which includes South Japan after [[Operation Golden Dawn]]). South Korea's geographic position — at the intersection of LETF Pacific holdings and the Chinese theater — makes it a valuable operations platform for the U.S. as it begins covert operations in China.

## Role in Operation Silent Halo

[[Operation Silent Halo]] (1962) — the U.S. covert operation supporting Chinese resistance against LETF occupation — is staged partly from South Korean territory. South Korea's proximity to both China and Japan (where LETF holds South Japan) makes it a logical forward operating base for:

- U.S. intelligence collection on LETF Chinese operations
- Logistical support for resistance networks operating in Chinese coastal zones
- Communication relays between U.S. command and Chinese resistance cells

South Korea's role in Silent Halo predates its own annexation, creating an ironic sequence: South Korea helps the U.S. support Chinese resistance, then is itself conquered and requires outside support for its own resistance.

## Multi-Target Nuclear Campaign

The [[Multi-Target Nuclear Campaign]] (1962) strikes Seoul — South Korea's capital and largest city — as part of the coordinated eleven-nation strike. The Seoul nuclear strike destroys South Korean government command infrastructure and eliminates organized military resistance capacity. The destruction of Seoul is particularly significant given the city's density and its role as the primary node of South Korean governance.

## Operation Total Fade and Annexation

[[Operation Total Fade]] (1962) advances LETF forces across East Asia. South Korea is seized through operations advancing from LETF-controlled North Asia and maritime approaches:

- LETF ground forces advance south across the Korean Peninsula from northern Korean territories
- Naval operations support coastal landings on the peninsula's western and eastern coasts
- Busan and Incheon are secured in the ground campaign's southern push

South Korea is declared the **[[Korean Fade Province]]** under LETF administrative governance. LETF control of the full Korean Peninsula consolidates its East Asian continental position.

## Resistance and Liberation

South Korea's resistance benefits from the U.S.'s continued strategic interest in the Korean Peninsula. As the U.S. pivots toward the anti-LETF counter-coalition in 1963, South Korea becomes one of the East Asian territories where U.S. support — now overt rather than covert — directly contributes to liberation operations. The Korean Fade Province is liberated with U.S. support during the collapse phase, restoring South Korean sovereignty.

South Korea's liberation arc has a geographic logic: the same maritime and logistical access that made it useful for Silent Halo staging makes it accessible for liberation operations. U.S. Pacific presence, even during the LETF's maximum overextension, maintains sufficient reach to support Korean operations.

## Legacy

South Korea's arc illustrates the campaign's recurring theme that U.S. strategic investment in a territory — whether through covert operations staging or liberation support — creates a connection that shapes the territory's endgame outcomes. The same relationship that made Korea a Silent Halo base eventually makes it a U.S. liberation priority, giving South Korea a recovery trajectory that reflects the depth of U.S.-Korean operational entanglement.
