The **People's Republic of La Plata (PRLP)** is the [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]]'s South American political foothold — a renamed and reorganized Argentina that operates as an LETF-aligned client state under National Socialist governance framing. The PRLP is unique among LETF-adjacent entities in that it is not directly annexed but instead reorganized as an aligned state, given nuclear weapons and financial aid, and used as the LETF's primary South American political presence. The PRLP creates regional tension with neighboring Brazil, Chile, and U.S.-backed factions, and becomes a central focus of the South American rollback crisis when internal instability and the [[Operation Silence of the South]] crackdown undermine its viability.

## Background

Argentina's transformation into the People's Republic of La Plata represents the LETF's preferred model for South American expansion: rather than direct military conquest and annexation, the LETF sponsors a political reorganization of Argentina's government, installs LETF-aligned National Socialist governance, and converts the renamed state into a regional power projection platform.

The renaming itself — "La Plata" referencing the Río de la Plata (the historical regional designation for the Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata) — frames the LETF's Argentine client state in terms of historical Latin American political identity rather than explicit LETF terminology, giving the arrangement a veneer of indigenous political legitimacy.

## LETF Support Package

The LETF's investment in the PRLP is substantial:

- **Five nuclear weapons**: The LETF transfers five tactical nuclear weapons to the PRLP's arsenal — a major strategic endowment that gives the PRLP deterrent capability against regional rivals and U.S.-backed opposition
- **Financial aid**: Significant LETF financial transfers support PRLP government operations, military modernization, and propaganda infrastructure
- **Political support**: The LETF provides diplomatic recognition and political backing for the PRLP's claims to represent legitimate Argentine governance

The nuclear transfer is particularly significant — it is one of the only cases in the campaign where the LETF deliberately spreads nuclear weapons to an aligned state rather than keeping them within LETF-controlled territory.

## Regional Impact and U.S. Opposition

The PRLP's nuclear-armed LETF alignment generates immediate regional tension:

- **Brazil** (partially occupied as [[Amazonian Fade Province]]) faces a LETF-aligned nuclear state on its southern border, complicating resistance operations in southern Brazil
- **Chile** is pressured by the PRLP's military expansion and LETF backing
- **U.S. covert operations**: The United States funds opposition movements within the PRLP, supporting anti-LETF Argentine factions that resist the National Socialist reorganization — one of the U.S.'s early signals that it maintains independent regional interests even while nominally aligned with the LETF

## Instability and Operation Silence of the South

The PRLP never achieves stable governance. The LETF's crackdown strategy — exemplified by [[Operation Silence of the South]] (1963), which targets the PRLP alongside occupied Brazil and Boombayah — is partly driven by the PRLP's persistent internal instability:

- U.S.-funded opposition movements within Argentina resist PRLP governance
- Anti-LETF Argentine factions coordinate with Brazilian Amazon resistance networks
- The PRLP's popular legitimacy is undermined by both LETF-style governance impositions and the visible failure of LETF South American management to deliver stability or prosperity

The Silence of the South crackdown targeting PRLP internal opposition backfires — the purges increase popular resentment rather than stabilizing the state, and the campaign's broader South American backlash accelerates the LETF's regional losses.

## Legacy

The PRLP is the campaign's demonstration that LETF-aligned client states are not more stable than directly annexed territories. The combination of nuclear armament, financial dependence on LETF, U.S. covert destabilization, and governance legitimacy failures makes the PRLP a persistent liability rather than a reliable asset. Its trajectory from model South American foothold to rollback crisis symbol mirrors the broader LETF experience across its overextended empire.
