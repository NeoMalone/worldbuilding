**Operation Nordic Dawn** is [[Sweden]]'s amphibious invasion of Corsica in 1961, conducted in the immediate aftermath of the [[Treaty of Paris]] (which formalized Sweden's occupation of eastern and southern France). With mainland France subdued, Corsica — France's largest Mediterranean island — becomes an opportunistic secondary objective. The island offers Sweden a naval hub directly in the Mediterranean, flanking both Sweden's French coastal holdings and the broader GLA operational theater in the Middle East and North Africa.

## Background

Following [[Operation Northern Fury]] and the Treaty of Paris, Sweden controls a large portion of mainland France including Marseille and Lyon. Corsica, though geographically separated, is a French territory with military airstrips and deep-water harbors. Sweden identifies it as a natural extension of its French holdings — and the first step toward a permanent Swedish Mediterranean presence.

France, significantly weakened, cannot mount a serious defense of the island.

## Plan and Execution

### Phase 1: Naval and Air Superiority

Swedish naval forces blockade Corsica from the mainland, preventing any French reinforcement. Swedish fighter jets secure air superiority over the island.

**Targets:**
- **Ajaccio** (Corsican capital) — Swedish bombers strike military installations and government buildings
- **Bastia** (northern Corsica) — Bombed to neutralize airstrips and logistical hubs

### Phase 2: Amphibious Assault

Swedish marines land on Corsica's western coast, advancing toward Ajaccio. Simultaneously, mechanized units land near Bastia to secure the northern region.

### Phase 3: Ground Operations

- **Ajaccio** — Urban combat as Swedish forces push into the city; falls after stiff local resistance
- **Bastia** — Falls quickly due to limited defenses; Sweden secures the northern port

## Outcome

| **Territory** | **Outcome** |
|---|---|
| Ajaccio | Swedish capital of Corsica; military and administrative hub |
| Bastia | Northern port secured; naval base established |

Corsica is declared a **Swedish Mediterranean Territory** — Sweden's first major overseas possession. Swedish casualties are minimal due to overwhelming air and naval superiority. France refuses to formally recognize the annexation, but its protests carry no enforcement weight.

## Aftermath: Corsica Under Sweden

Sweden rapidly establishes military bases in Ajaccio and Bastia, using the island as a naval hub for Mediterranean operations. Two flagship IKEA stores are later built in Ajaccio and Bastia during the 1962 IKEA expansion wave — a characteristically Swedish gesture combining military occupation with economic integration.

France's continued non-recognition of Swedish sovereignty over Corsica becomes a source of persistent friction, fueling resistance movements within the island's population.

## Global Reactions

- **NATO**: Raises concerns about Sweden's growing territorial ambitions; the capture of Corsica suggests Sweden's long-term goals extend beyond mainland France.
- **USSR**: Notes the strategic positioning of a Swedish naval base in the central Mediterranean.
- **United Nations**: Protests but cannot intervene.

## Legacy

Operation Nordic Dawn establishes Sweden's Mediterranean footprint and provides the naval platform from which Sweden projects power south during the broader GLA campaigns of 1962. The island's acquisition forms part of a broader Swedish expansion pattern — France → Corsica → East Germany ([[Operation Nordic Eclipse]]) — that makes Sweden the dominant European power within the Golden Lemons Alliance.
