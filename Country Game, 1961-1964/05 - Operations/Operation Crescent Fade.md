**Operation Crescent Fade** is the joint [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]] and [[United States of America]] campaign to annex Syria in 1961. Executed in the immediate aftermath of [[Operation Cedar Fade]]'s conquest of Lebanon, it represents the third consecutive Levant campaign in the LETF's rapid 1961 expansion arc. The United States contributes major naval and air assets — aircraft carriers, bombers, and amphibious landing craft — deployed to the Syrian coast at Lattakia and Tartus, without any U.S. intent to annex Syrian territory.

## Background

Having secured Israel and Lebanon, the LETF identifies Syria as the next critical link in its Middle Eastern empire. Control of Syria would give the LETF a land bridge connecting Egypt through the Levant to Iraq, and provide strategic depth against any northern threat. The United States supports the operation as part of its ongoing GLA commitments, providing firepower far exceeding what was deployed in Lebanon.

[[Magnus Maximus]] commands the operation. [[Alexander the Great (Fictional LETF Role)]] advises on the multi-axis approach, drawing on historical precedents from the ancient Syrian campaigns.

## Plan and Execution

### Phase 1: Airstrikes and Naval Bombardments

- **Damascus** — Strategic government buildings and military bases targeted by LETF Su-24 bombers
- **Homs and Aleppo** — Bombed to disrupt Syrian military coordination and supply lines
- **Lattakia and Tartus** — U.S. Navy launches missile strikes from carrier groups positioned off the Syrian coast, neutralizing coastal defenses and enabling amphibious landing preparation

### Phase 2: Ground Invasion

**Southern Offensive (LETF from Lebanon):**
LETF mechanized units advance north from the Lebanese border, capturing **Homs** and pushing south toward Damascus from multiple angles.

**Central Offensive (Joint LETF-U.S.):**
Amphibious landings at **Tartus** and **Lattakia** secure the entire coastal region and open an overland route toward Damascus from the west.

### Phase 3: Urban Combat

**Damascus** — The capital holds out longer than any previous Levant target, with intense street battles causing significant casualties on both sides. LETF forces — reinforced by the western coastal landing — eventually encircle and capture the city, which becomes the administrative center of the occupation.

**Aleppo** — Falls after combined air and ground pressure from the north.

## Outcome

| **Territory** | **Outcome** |
|---|---|
| Damascus | LETF administrative capital; hub of the Syrian Province of the Fade |
| Tartus and Lattakia | Secured by U.S. naval forces; transferred to LETF for consolidation |
| Homs | Strategic supply node secured early in the campaign |
| Aleppo | Taken after combined pressure; key northern city absorbed |

Syria is declared the **Syrian Province of the Fade**. The U.S. provides overwhelming support but accepts no territorial gain, transferring all occupied areas to LETF administration upon campaign completion.

## Casualties

Syrian resistance inflicts moderate losses on LETF forces, making this the costliest single Levant campaign to date. The LETF's supply lines are stretched following the rapid Israel → Lebanon → Syria sequence, and the operation marks the first visible signs of the logistical strain that will contribute to later overextension.

## Global Reactions

- **United Nations**: Condemns the annexation but struggles to respond effectively.
- **USSR**: Heightened mobilization; the Soviet Union deploys forces along its southern borders in response to the LETF's sweep through the Middle East.
- **China**: Issues strong protests against U.S. involvement but refrains from direct military action.

## Legacy

Operation Crescent Fade completes the LETF's Levant arc — Israel, Lebanon, Syria — in a matter of months. Together with the near-simultaneous annexation of Jordan ([[Operation Fade's Crossing]]), it gives the LETF a contiguous land empire stretching from Egypt through the entire eastern Mediterranean to the Iraqi border. The operation also represents the high-water mark of U.S.-LETF military cooperation before internal GLA tensions begin to develop over the LETF's use of weapons of mass destruction and genocidal campaigns in 1962.
