**Operation Baltic Fade** is the [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]]'s campaign against Poland in 1961, launched in the immediate aftermath of the [[Operation Crimson Fade]] Conditional Surrender with the USSR. Having secured Ukraine and the Caucasus, the LETF turns to Poland to capture **Kaliningrad** — the strategic Baltic enclave — and to establish direct access to Baltic Sea ports. The campaign ends not in total annexation but in a conditional surrender that leaves Warsaw and Krakow under Polish governance while the LETF absorbs the eastern and northern territories it desires.

## Background

Kaliningrad, the formerly German Baltic exclave surrounded by Soviet and Polish territory, becomes strategically critical once the LETF secures Ukraine. Its capture would give the LETF a Baltic Sea port and further isolate remaining European states. [[Magnus Maximus]] approves the campaign; forces advance from the newly acquired Caspian Province and through allied Czechoslovakia.

## Plan and Execution

### Phase 1: Air Superiority and Bombardments

- **Warsaw** — Bombed by Su-24s to cripple Poland's command infrastructure
- **Gdansk and Gdynia** — Targeted to disrupt Polish naval operations
- **Kaliningrad** — Bombed to weaken defenses in and around the city

### Phase 2: Ground Invasion

**Northern Push (LETF from eastern Ukraine):**
Mechanized infantry and tank divisions advance toward Kaliningrad and move toward Gdansk and the Baltic coast.

**Central Push (from Czechoslovakia):**
Forces advance from allied Czechoslovakia, capturing **Krakow** and moving toward Warsaw.

**Eastern Push:**
LETF forces move into **Bialystok** and **Lublin**, cutting off Polish reinforcements and isolating Warsaw from the east.

### Phase 3: Urban Combat

- **Warsaw** — LETF forces engage in intense street battles; the city faces stiff resistance from Polish defenders and sustains significant damage
- **Kaliningrad** — Secured after prolonged fighting; declared a key administrative and military hub

## Conditional Surrender and Territorial Settlement

Following the campaign, the LETF and Poland negotiate a conditional surrender:

**LETF gains:**
- **Kaliningrad** — Fully annexed; renamed the **Baltic Province of the Fade**
- **Eastern Poland** — Regions surrounding Bialystok and Lublin absorbed into LETF territory
- **Northern Corridor** — Gdansk and Gdynia secured; LETF gains Baltic Sea port access

**Poland retains:**
- **Warsaw** — Remains under Polish control, though geographically surrounded by LETF and Swedish territories
- **Krakow** — Retained as cultural and administrative hub for the surviving Polish government

**Terms:**
1. Demilitarized zone established along new borders
2. Poland supplies grain and coal to the LETF for five years in exchange for peace
3. Poland reduces military activity near LETF borders
4. A demilitarized buffer zone is established

## Global Reactions

- **United Nations**: Condemns the agreement; calls for restoration of pre-war borders; no action taken.
- **NATO**: Heightened alarm — the fall of portions of Poland is seen as a stepping stone for further LETF expansion into Central Europe.
- **USSR**: The Soviets watch closely; the LETF now surrounds Poland from multiple sides, with Sweden also holding territory in the Baltic region.
- **Sweden**: Notes with satisfaction the LETF's complementary Baltic presence, which further isolates Poland from western support.

## Legacy

Operation Baltic Fade completes the LETF's encirclement of Central Europe. Combined with Sweden's French occupation and Austria's Central European presence, the Golden Lemons Alliance now effectively controls the continent's strategic geography. The Baltic Province of the Fade — Kaliningrad — becomes one of the LETF's key naval assets in Europe, enabling naval pressure in the Baltic and positioning the empire for operations against Scandinavia and Northern Europe if desired.
