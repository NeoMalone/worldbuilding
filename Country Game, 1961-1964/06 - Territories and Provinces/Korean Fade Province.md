The **Korean Fade Province** is the LETF's administrative designation for occupied [[South Korea]] following the 1962 [[Operation Total Fade]] annexation and the nuclear strike on Seoul during the [[Multi-Target Nuclear Campaign]]. The province completes LETF control of the entire Korean Peninsula, joining the previously secured northern Korean territories. Korea's liberation during the collapse phase, supported overtly by U.S. forces as the [[Golden Lemons Alliance]] pivots against the LETF, is one of the cleaner East Asian liberation outcomes.

## Geography and Administration

The province covers South Korean national territory under Morganist institutional governance. Following Seoul's nuclear devastation, provincial administration is distributed across surviving major cities — primarily Busan (the country's second city and major southern port) and Incheon (port adjacent to Seoul). LETF garrison forces concentrate on coastal and urban zones.

## Strategic Value

The Korean Peninsula's strategic value to the LETF is significant:
- **East Asian continental position**: Combined North-South Korean control gives the LETF the entire Korean Peninsula
- **Pacific positioning**: Korean coastal access supports LETF Pacific operations adjacent to South Japan
- **Chinese border proximity**: Northern Korean territory borders Chinese territories under [[Sino Fade Provinces]] administration
- **Naval logistics**: Busan and Incheon ports support LETF maritime operations

## Pre-Annexation U.S. Connection

Before Korean Fade Province annexation, South Korea hosted U.S. covert operations supporting Chinese resistance via [[Operation Silent Halo]] (1962). This pre-existing U.S. operational connection becomes strategically important in the collapse-phase liberation arc — the U.S. retains intelligence, logistical, and operational connections to the Korean Peninsula even after annexation.

## Liberation with U.S. Support

During the collapse phase, U.S. support for Korean liberation is more direct than for most other occupied territories. Several factors enable this:
- **Maritime access**: U.S. Pacific naval presence enables direct support operations
- **Geographic reach**: South Korea is accessible from U.S. bases in the broader Pacific theater
- **Pre-existing intelligence**: U.S. intelligence networks operating from Silent Halo continue functioning during the occupation period
- **Strategic priority**: Korean Peninsula liberation matters to U.S. East Asian strategic positioning

The result is one of the more directly U.S.-supported liberation outcomes in the campaign — Korean restoration of sovereignty draws on indigenous resistance capability augmented by overt U.S. operational support.
