## Detailed Canon Account

USSR: The Global Speaker Project The USSR, in a bold move to assert technological and cultural
dominance, launches a project to create a speaker system capable of transmitting sound across the
entire planet.

### The purpose is to broadcast two bizarre phrases on repeat

1. "Imagine if a ninja got a low taper fade." 2. "Hey everyone, stop being suspicious of bakas."
Additionally, the USSR issues a formal apology to all countries they've invaded and extends olive
branches to their enemies, including the USA.

### The Speaker System

1. Design and Technology:

The system relies on an array of experimental sound amplifiers and low- frequency transmitters
positioned across the USSR, including Siberia and newly annexed Cuba. Engineers struggle with the
physics of transmitting sound waves over such vast distances without severe distortion, but the
Kremlin insists on success.

2. Challenges:

Technical Hurdles: The required energy and infrastructure strain Soviet resources.
Early tests result in widespread power outages across Moscow and neighboring
cities.
Message Reception: Many Soviet scientists express quiet embarrassment at the
phrases being broadcast, viewing the project as a waste of resources.
United States: Counterintelligence via Number Stations
Learning about the Soviet speaker project, the U.S. launches Operation Silent Sky, an
effort to establish a network of number stations and radio broadcasts to counter and
sabotage the Soviet plan.

### Key Actions

1. Spy Network Setup:

The CIA mobilizes operatives to infiltrate Soviet facilities working on the speaker project. Spies
in Siberia and Cuba gather intelligence on the amplifier arrays and their vulnerabilities.

2. Number Stations:

U.S. military bases and covert installations begin broadcasting cryptic messages on shortwave radio
frequencies, using randomized sequences of numbers and letters to confuse Soviet intelligence. These
broadcasts include fake Soviet instructions designed to disrupt internal operations, such as
rerouting materials to nonexistent locations.

3. Sabotage Efforts:

A covert team infiltrates a key facility in Novosibirsk, planting malware in the control systems for
the amplifiers. This causes feedback loops during test broadcasts, damaging equipment and delaying
progress.

### Outcome of the Speaker Project

1. Partial Success:

The USSR manages a test broadcast across Eastern Europe and parts of Siberia, where garbled versions
of the phrases can be heard. However, severe distortion makes the messages nearly incomprehensible.
Power grids in several Soviet cities collapse under the strain, causing widespread outages.

2. U.S. Countermeasures:

The sabotage significantly delays the project, forcing Soviet engineers to focus on repairs rather
than expanding the system. Number stations sow chaos within the Soviet communication network, with
conflicting orders slowing progress further.

3. Global Perception:

The apology issued by the USSR is overshadowed by the absurdity of the speaker project. Western
media mock the broadcasts, dubbing them the "Baka Broadcasts." The Soviet public grows increasingly
disillusioned, questioning the priorities of their government.

### Summary of Events

1. USSR:

Launches an ambitious but flawed speaker project, with limited success and significant resource
strain. Apologies to enemies are received with skepticism, given the simultaneous continuation of
absurd projects.

2. USA:

Successfully sabotages the Soviet speaker project through espionage and counterintelligence,
delaying its expansion. Establishes a robust network of number stations to confuse Soviet operatives
and strengthen U.S. spy networks.

3. Global Impact:

The Soviet Union faces increased ridicule and isolation, while the U.S. gains ground in the
intelligence war. Neutral nations begin distancing themselves from the USSR, viewing its leadership
as erratic.
