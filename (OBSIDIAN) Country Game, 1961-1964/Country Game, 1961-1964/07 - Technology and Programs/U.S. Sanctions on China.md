The **U.S. Sanctions on China** are the 1962 [[United States of America]] trade restrictions and naval pressure measures targeting Chinese military equipment, semiconductor, and electronics trade routes. Implemented before the LETF's [[Operation Jade Fade]] invasion of China, the sanctions deliberately weaken Chinese defensive capability ahead of LETF military action — making them one of the campaign's clearest examples of GLA member coordination through differentiated capability deployment, with the U.S. softening targets economically that the LETF then conquers militarily.

## Scope

The sanctions target specific categories of Chinese trade:

- **Military equipment**: Restrictions on weapons-related imports degrading Chinese conventional military modernization
- **Semiconductors**: Blocking access to advanced semiconductor technology, limiting Chinese electronic and computing capability development
- **Electronics**: Broader electronics trade restrictions affecting Chinese industrial and consumer technology

## Naval Enforcement

The sanctions are enforced through U.S. naval pressure across critical Asian maritime corridors:

- **South China Sea**: U.S. naval presence monitors and intercepts trade traffic
- **Strait of Malacca**: Critical maritime chokepoint where U.S. forces enforce sanctions on shipping into and out of Chinese ports

The naval enforcement transforms the sanctions from paper restrictions into operationally enforced measures.

## Strategic Function

The sanctions serve multiple strategic purposes:

- **Pre-invasion softening**: Reduce Chinese defensive capability ahead of [[Operation Jade Fade]]'s LETF invasion, ensuring more rapid LETF success
- **GLA coordination**: Demonstrate the alliance's ability to combine economic and military instruments — U.S. sanctions weaken targets that LETF military force then conquers
- **U.S. independent leverage**: Maintain U.S. independent strategic interests in East Asia even within GLA framework
- **Hedging**: The sanctions also signal U.S. willingness to act against Chinese interests independently, providing flexibility for later strategic adjustment

## Pre-Operation Jade Fade Context

The sanctions' timing — before Operation Jade Fade — reveals the GLA's coordinated approach to China:
- 1962 (early): U.S. sanctions weaken China economically
- 1962 (mid): LETF invades militarily, exploiting weakened defenses
- 1962 (post-invasion): U.S. covertly supports Chinese resistance via [[Operation Silent Halo]]

This sequence — sanctions, invasion, then covert resistance support — demonstrates the U.S.'s complex and ultimately self-contradictory strategic position vis-à-vis China.

## Strategic Self-Contradiction

The sanctions represent one of the campaign's clearer examples of U.S. strategic self-contradiction:
- The U.S. sanctions weaken China to support LETF conquest
- The U.S. then covertly supports Chinese resistance against the LETF conquest
- This contradiction illustrates the U.S. transition from active GLA partner to anti-LETF actor

## Legacy

The sanctions' legacy extends beyond their immediate effect on Chinese capability. They establish a precedent for U.S. economic-military coordination with allied military operations and demonstrate the strategic flexibility the U.S. maintains within the GLA framework. The sanctions also contribute to the conditions under which the LETF's eventual Chinese overextension produces strategic catastrophe — a Chinese state weakened by sanctions and then conquered by the LETF proves much more difficult to administer than either power anticipated.
