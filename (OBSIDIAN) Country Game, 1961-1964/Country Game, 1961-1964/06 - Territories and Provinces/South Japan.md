**South Japan** is the southern Japanese territorial zone annexed by the [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]] following the GLA conquest of Japan in [[Operation Golden Dawn]] (1961). Unlike Austria's [[North Japan]], which retains significant local institutional frameworks, South Japan is administered under full Morganist imposition. The territory provides the LETF with its primary Pacific continental presence and integrates with Hawaiian holdings ([[Pacific Province of the Fade]]) to form the LETF's broader Pacific operational network.

## Geography and Administration

The territory covers the southern portion of the Japanese archipelago:
- **Kansai region**: Osaka, Kyoto — major urban and cultural centers
- **Chugoku region**: Hiroshima — western Honshu administrative center
- **Kyushu region**: Fukuoka, Nagasaki — southern Japan administrative and industrial centers

LETF provincial governance imposes Morganist institutional frameworks across former Japanese governmental, educational, and cultural institutions. Tokyo's southern districts (the northern districts being under Austrian administration) serve as LETF's regional administrative hub.

## Strategic Value

South Japan provides multiple critical strategic assets to the LETF:
- **Pacific continental position**: Forward operating base on the Pacific Rim
- **Industrial capacity**: Japanese industrial infrastructure converted to LETF military-industrial production
- **Naval logistics**: Pacific port infrastructure supports LETF Pacific maritime operations
- **Korean Peninsula proximity**: Adjacent to Korean operations and the eventual [[Korean Fade Province]]
- **Pacific surveillance**: Coordinated with the broader LETF Pacific intelligence network

## Resistance

Japanese resistance in South Japan operates with characteristics shaped by occupation conditions:
- Cultural resistance to Morganist imposition draws on strong Japanese national and religious identity
- Underground networks form in major cities, particularly in cultural centers like Kyoto
- Geographic proximity to Austrian-administered North Japan provides opportunities for cross-border coordination during the late-period anti-LETF pivot

## Late-Period Dynamics

During the LETF's collapse phase, South Japan's position becomes strategically complex:
- LETF garrison forces are progressively reduced as resources are reallocated to core empire defense
- Resistance networks expand from underground positions
- Coordination with Austrian assets in North Japan creates favorable conditions for liberation

The eventual restoration of Japanese sovereignty across both former occupation zones represents one of the campaign's Pacific-region resolution narratives.
