The **Amazonian Fade Province** is the LETF's administrative designation for occupied [[Brazil]] following the 1962 [[Operation Total Fade]] annexation campaign — preceded by a nuclear strike on São Paulo as part of the [[Multi-Target Nuclear Campaign]]. The province nominally covers Brazilian national territory under Morganist governance, but in practice LETF administrative authority extends only over coastal urban centers; the Amazon rainforest interior — covering more than half of Brazilian territory — remains an effective resistance sanctuary throughout the occupation.

## Geography and Administration

The province's administrative geography is sharply divided:

- **Coastal urban zone**: LETF garrison authority is concentrated in coastal Brazilian cities (Rio de Janeiro, Recife, Fortaleza, Belém) and accessible inland centers
- **Amazon interior**: The rainforest's geographic depth and density make sustained LETF presence impossible; this zone functions as a permanent resistance sanctuary
- **São Paulo crater zone**: The former largest city, devastated by nuclear strike, is partially abandoned and serves as a no-man's-land between coastal LETF authority and inland resistance

LETF provincial governance is administered from Rio de Janeiro under Morganist institutional frameworks. Local Brazilian institutions are dismantled or absorbed.

## Strategic Value

The province's strategic value to the LETF is primarily symbolic and economic — Brazil's eliminated independence eliminates the strategic anchor of any potential South American resistance coalition. Coastal port infrastructure provides Atlantic naval logistics. Resource extraction operations target Brazilian commodities. However, the practical economic value is undermined by the constant operational costs of resistance suppression that never succeeds.

## Resistance and Suppression

Amazon-based resistance networks operate throughout the occupation, becoming one of the largest and most geographically protected resistance theaters in the campaign. [[Operation Silence of the South]] (1963) targets these networks specifically but achieves limited results. Brazilian resistance networks coordinate with [[Boombayah]] sanctuary operations and Argentine opposition to the [[People's Republic of La Plata (PRLP)]].

## Liberation

During the collapse phase, Amazon resistance networks expand from rainforest sanctuaries into coastal cities as LETF garrison forces weaken. Brazil's liberation arc is one of the more rapid territorial restorations in the collapse period, driven by sustained indigenous resistance capability built up across the occupation period.
