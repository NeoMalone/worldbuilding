**East Germany** (German Democratic Republic, GDR) is the Soviet-aligned German state annexed by [[Sweden]] during [[Operation Nordic Eclipse]] (1962). Coming after Sweden's conquest of West Germany's territory under [[Operation Total Fade]] and the earlier Swedish control of France under the [[Treaty of Paris]], East Germany's annexation gives Sweden effective administrative control over the entire German-speaking central European zone. The operation secures Berlin, Leipzig, Dresden, and Erfurt and declares the territory the **Germanic Province of Sweden**. In the campaign's later arc, East and West Germany's territories are gradually reunified into a German resistance bloc as Swedish administrative capacity deteriorates.

## Background

East Germany in the early 1960s campaign timeline is a Soviet-aligned state whose political legitimacy depends entirely on continued Soviet power. As [[Operation Crimson Fade]] (1961) and then [[Operation Soviet Eclipse]] (1962) progressively eliminate Soviet capacity, East Germany's government loses its primary political patron and military guarantor. The state becomes increasingly vulnerable to Swedish expansion, which Sweden had already demonstrated was feasible by successfully annexing adjacent German-speaking territories.

Sweden's annexation of East Germany is also strategically motivated by the desire to complete German territorial unification under Swedish control — eliminating the divided German zone as a potential future power center.

## Operation Nordic Eclipse

[[Operation Nordic Eclipse]] (1962) is Sweden's focused annexation campaign targeting East Germany. The operation follows Sweden's successful expansion into West German territory:

- **Berlin**: Sweden's primary target — the divided city is seized in combined ground and air operations; East Berlin falls, completing Swedish control over the full city
- **Leipzig**: Major industrial and cultural center secured; Swedish forces advance from the north and west
- **Dresden**: Historically significant city secured; Swedish occupation established
- **Erfurt**: Central German administrative center secured; Swedish provincial governance installed

East Germany's military — without Soviet backing and facing a militarily superior Sweden operating with U.S. logistical support — offers conventional resistance that is overcome within the operation's primary timeframe.

## Germanic Province of Sweden

Following the annexation, East Germany is incorporated into the **Germanic Province of Sweden** — Sweden's administrative designation for its German territorial holdings. Swedish provincial governance:

- Imposes Swedish administrative and economic frameworks over former GDR institutions
- Integrates East German industrial capacity into Swedish supply chains
- Faces ongoing resistance from German nationalist and socialist factions who reject Swedish occupation regardless of their previous anti-Western positions
- Must reconcile East German population attitudes shaped by decades of Soviet-style governance with Swedish governance frameworks

The Germanic Province, covering both former East and West German territories, represents Sweden's largest and most complex occupied territory — a massive expansion of Swedish administrative responsibility that contributes to its own version of overextension pressure.

## Reunification Arc

Later in the campaign timeline, as Swedish administrative capacity is strained by overextension pressures and the LETF's collapse creates instability across Central Europe, East and West German resistance movements — previously operating independently under different political frameworks — begin coordinating. The eventual emergence of a reunified German resistance bloc is one of the Central European resistance success stories of the 1963 collapse period.

This de facto reunification is ironic: Sweden's annexation of both German states, intended to prevent German power from re-emerging, instead creates the conditions for German resistance to coordinate across the old East-West divide.

## Legacy

East Germany's annexation completes Sweden's Central European territorial arc — from France in the west to Germany in the center. However, the Germanic Province illustrates the recurring campaign theme that territorial control does not produce stable governance. East Germany's resistance communities, shaped by their own experience of Soviet occupation and now facing a different form of foreign control, contribute substantially to the Central European resistance networks that eventually challenge Swedish and LETF administrative authority in 1963.
