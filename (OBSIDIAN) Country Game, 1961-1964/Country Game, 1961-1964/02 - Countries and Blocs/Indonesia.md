**Indonesia** is a Southeast Asian archipelago nation annexed by the [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]] during [[Operation Total Fade]] (1962) following a nuclear strike on Jakarta as part of the [[Multi-Target Nuclear Campaign]]. Declared the **[[Archipelago Fade Province]]**, Indonesia's thousands of islands create an inherently decentralized occupation challenge that the LETF cannot effectively administer. During the collapse phase, the Archipelago Fade Province fragments into smaller anti-LETF states as different islands and island chains reassert autonomous or independent governance, making Indonesia's liberation a process of fragmentation rather than unified national restoration.

## Background

Indonesia's geographic character — an archipelago of over 17,000 islands spanning a vast maritime zone — makes it fundamentally different from continental occupation targets. Any occupation of Indonesia requires sustained naval presence across enormous distances, and the dispersal of population across thousands of islands means no single strike can neutralize the territory's resistance capacity.

The LETF targets Indonesia as part of Operation Total Fade's simultaneous annexation wave; the Jakarta nuclear strike is intended to collapse centralized Indonesian governance before the conventional invasion, but the archipelago's decentralized character means effective governance was never entirely dependent on Jakarta to begin with.

## Multi-Target Nuclear Campaign

The [[Multi-Target Nuclear Campaign]] (1962) strikes Jakarta — Indonesia's capital and largest city — as part of the coordinated eleven-nation strike package. Jakarta's destruction collapses central government infrastructure and eliminates organized military command capacity. However, the nuclear strike's impact on Indonesia is geographically limited in a way that is not true of more centralized nations: Indonesian regional governance across Sumatra, Kalimantan, Sulawesi, Papua, and the eastern islands continues even as Jakarta falls.

## Operation Total Fade and Annexation

[[Operation Total Fade]] (1962) targets Indonesia's major islands through combined naval and amphibious operations:

- Java (including Jakarta, Surabaya, Bandung) is secured first as the most densely populated and economically critical island
- Sumatra is seized through northern and southern amphibious landings
- Kalimantan and Sulawesi face more limited LETF garrisons given resource constraints
- Outer islands receive nominal LETF administrative presence or are effectively ungoverned

Indonesia is declared the **[[Archipelago Fade Province]]** — but the designation masks a significant administrative reality: the LETF controls major Java and Sumatra urban centers but has minimal practical presence across the archipelago's outer thousands of islands.

## Archipelago Resistance and Fragmentation

Indonesia's resistance takes a form unique among LETF occupation zones: rather than unified national resistance, it manifests as island-by-island autonomy. Islands and island chains with minimal LETF presence gradually reassert de facto independence. This fragmentation process accelerates during the collapse phase:

- Outer Kalimantan, Sulawesi, Maluku, and Papua regions never fully submit to LETF governance
- Local resistance networks — organized around existing regional, ethnic, and religious identities — resist Morganist imposition effectively
- As LETF naval presence thins during overextension, maritime patrol gaps allow inter-island resistance coordination to develop

The collapse phase sees the Archipelago Fade Province disintegrate into a patchwork of:
- Formally restored Indonesian national territory (Java and Sumatra coastal centers)
- Autonomous regional states that resist reintegration into any single national framework
- Anti-LETF militias operating from outer island sanctuaries

## Legacy

Indonesia's arc illustrates how an archipelago's geographic character can defeat an occupation even when that occupation has successfully seized nominal control. The LETF could take Jakarta and call it the Archipelago Fade Province; it could not take 17,000 islands. Indonesia's fragmentation during the collapse phase is one of the campaign's clearest demonstrations that the gap between conquest and governance is widest in geographically complex territories.
