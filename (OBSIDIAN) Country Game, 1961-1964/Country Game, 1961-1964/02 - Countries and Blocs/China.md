**China** is the [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]]'s largest and most strategically significant conquest in the 1962 expansion arc. Preceded by U.S. sanctions designed to weaken Chinese military and industrial capacity, China is invaded in [[Operation Jade Fade]] — a three-front LETF campaign advancing from Mongolia, the eastern coast, and the south. Despite its conquest, China proves impossible to fully pacify: its population scale, geographic depth, and industrial resilience sustain active resistance networks throughout the occupation period, supported covertly by the United States via [[Operation Silent Halo]]. China's occupation is the single largest contributor to the LETF's overextension crisis.

## Background

In the 1961–1962 period, China occupies a complex strategic position in the campaign timeline. The United States, as a GLA member, applies economic and military pressure on China through targeted sanctions — blocking semiconductor trade and restricting military supply chains — in a deliberate effort to reduce Chinese defensive capability ahead of anticipated LETF military action. Whether the U.S. sanctions are coordinated with the LETF or represent independent American maneuvering is left ambiguous in the source material, but their practical effect is to weaken China's ability to respond to the 1962 invasion.

## Operation Jade Fade

[[Operation Jade Fade]] (1962) is the LETF's invasion of China, executed on three simultaneous fronts:

- **Northern front** — Advancing from the [[Mongolian Province of the Fade]] (previously annexed Mongolia), LETF forces push into Inner Mongolia and northern China, targeting Beijing and the northern industrial corridor
- **Eastern front** — LETF naval and amphibious forces advance toward Shanghai and the eastern coast, striking China's most economically productive coastal regions
- **Southern front** — LETF forces based in occupied Vietnam and Southeast Asia push northward through Yunnan and Guangdong, targeting Guangzhou and the Pearl River Delta

The scale of the campaign is enormous — China's size means LETF forces must spread across thousands of kilometers of contested territory. Major cities fall sequentially, but the Chinese military fragments rather than surrendering en masse, with regional commands retreating into interior and mountainous zones to sustain resistance.

## Occupation and Suppression

Following conquest, China is declared the **[[Sino Fade Provinces]]** — a group of administratively divided LETF territories nominally governed through Morganist doctrinal frameworks. However, the LETF's capacity to actually administer a territory of China's scale is severely limited. Supply chains are overextended, garrison forces are insufficient, and local governance structures resist integration.

[[Operation Jade Suppression]] (1962) is the LETF's attempt to consolidate control through mass population suppression across the Sino Fade Provinces. The campaign involves large-scale crackdowns on resistance infrastructure, population movement controls, and systematic targeting of suspected resistance leaders and networks. At the strategic level, the suppression campaign temporarily reduces organized resistance in major urban centers but generates catastrophic backlash — driving resistance deeper into China's vast interior and rural regions, where LETF forces cannot effectively operate.

The suppression campaign becomes one of the most cited examples of LETF overextension and is heavily used in international condemnation narratives that the [[Golden Lemons Alliance]] later assembles to justify [[Operation Silent Fade]].

## Operation Silent Halo

[[Operation Silent Halo]] (1962) is the United States' covert response to the Jade Suppression campaign — an ironic pivot given U.S. complicity in the pre-invasion sanctions. Motivated by domestic political pressure and strategic calculation that a LETF-dominated China represents a long-term threat to U.S. Pacific interests, the U.S. deploys covert support for Chinese resistance networks:

- Funding and supplies are channeled to hospital defenders and civilian resistance networks in occupied Chinese territory
- U.S. intelligence frames Iran as a weapons supplier to the LETF's Chinese operations — a disinformation campaign designed to create diplomatic complications for the LETF
- Operations are staged from Taiwan and South Korea, which remain outside direct LETF control

Silent Halo does not reverse the conquest, but it sustains resistance infrastructure and signals to the LETF that Chinese occupation will carry continuous costs.

## Strategic Consequences

China's conquest and resistance arc has several major strategic consequences for the campaign:

- **LETF overextension**: China alone requires more garrison forces than the LETF can sustainably deploy; combined with simultaneous occupations of India, Brazil, Germany, and others ([[Operation Total Fade]]), China's occupation is the largest single factor in LETF administrative collapse
- **International condemnation**: The Jade Suppression campaign becomes a primary exhibit in anti-LETF international advocacy, accelerating GLA counter-alliance formation
- **Resistance model**: Chinese resistance — decentralized, geographically distributed, sustained by interior geography — becomes the model that other occupied territories adapt; LETF counter-resistance campaigns ([[Operation Shadow Purge]]) directly cite Chinese networks as the benchmark threat
- **U.S. pivot**: Silent Halo marks the moment the U.S. begins covertly working against LETF operations while still nominally a GLA member — the first signal of the strategic pivot that culminates in [[Operation Silent Fade]]

## Legacy

China's role in the campaign is that of the empire's breaking point. No single conquest more thoroughly exposes the LETF's inability to convert military victory into stable governance. By the time the rollback begins with [[Operation Iron Dawn]], China's resistance networks are among the most active and capable in the former Soviet and Asian theaters. China's experience — conquest followed by suppression followed by deepening resistance — is the template the campaign uses to illustrate why overextension is fatal to the LETF's long-term viability.
