**Iraq** is one of the LETF's 1961 Middle Eastern conquests, seized in [[Operation Mesopotamian Fade]] — a joint LETF-U.S. operation targeting Baghdad, Basra, Mosul, and Kirkuk. Iraq's annexation as the **Mesopotamian Province of the Fade** gives the LETF control over a major oil-producing corridor, Persian Gulf access, and strategic land routes connecting the Levant to Iran, Turkey, and the Arabian Peninsula. Iraq serves as a critical staging ground for the LETF's subsequent regional expansion operations.

## Background

Iraq in the early 1961 campaign period is a major Middle Eastern state with substantial oil reserves, Persian Gulf port access, and geographic centrality between the Levant and Central Asia. The LETF's Middle Eastern expansion strategy requires Iraq as a connecting link — without control over the Mesopotamian corridor, the LETF's ability to project force eastward toward Iran and Turkey, or southward toward Saudi Arabia, is limited.

The United States' joint participation in Operation Mesopotamian Fade reflects the GLA's shared interest in securing Persian Gulf energy infrastructure and eliminating a Middle Eastern state that might otherwise become a base for resistance to GLA expansion.

## Operation Mesopotamian Fade

[[Operation Mesopotamian Fade]] (1961) is a joint LETF-U.S. campaign targeting Iraq's four major urban centers simultaneously:

- **Baghdad**: Iraq's capital is surrounded and besieged; LETF ground forces advance from the west while U.S. air power neutralizes air defenses; the city falls after urban combat
- **Basra**: Iraq's Persian Gulf port city is secured through combined naval and ground operations; the U.S. deploys carrier-based air support; Basra's port infrastructure is seized intact for LETF logistical use
- **Mosul**: Northern Iraq's major city is taken through ground advance; oil infrastructure in the Mosul region is a primary objective
- **Kirkuk**: The Kirkuk oil fields — among the most strategically valuable in the region — are secured as a specific LETF economic objective

Iraq's military, facing simultaneous pressure from two directions with no external allies available to respond, falls within the operation's timeframe.

## Strategic Value

Iraq's annexation as the **Mesopotamian Province of the Fade** gives the LETF several strategic assets:

- **Oil production**: Kirkuk and Basra oil fields supply LETF with domestic petroleum production that reduces dependence on external energy supply chains
- **Persian Gulf access**: Basra port gives LETF direct Persian Gulf naval access, enabling power projection toward the Arabian Sea and Indian Ocean
- **Land corridors**: The Mesopotamian plain provides overland routes connecting LETF Levantine holdings to future operations in Iran ([[Operation Total Fade]]) and Turkey ([[Operation Anatolian Fade]])
- **Staging capacity**: Baghdad's infrastructure is converted into a LETF military-administrative hub for regional operations; Mesopotamia serves as the logistics base for the Anatolian campaign launching against Turkey

## Legacy

Iraq's conquest is a foundational element of LETF Middle Eastern hegemony. The Mesopotamian Province's oil production and Gulf access give the LETF economic and naval resources that feed the empire's subsequent expansion. In the campaign's broader arc, Iraq represents one of the 1961 acquisitions that makes the LETF's 1962 global expansion logistically possible — a critical link in the chain of conquests that eventually leads to Total Fade.
