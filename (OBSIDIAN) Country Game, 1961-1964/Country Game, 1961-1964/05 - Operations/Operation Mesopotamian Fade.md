**Operation Mesopotamian Fade** is the joint [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]] and [[United States of America]] invasion of Iraq in 1961. The campaign secures the oil-rich Mesopotamian region, provides the LETF with a critical eastern staging ground for the subsequent campaign against Turkey ([[Operation Anatolian Fade]]), and opens Iraq's Persian Gulf ports to LETF naval expansion. The United States provides air and naval support without intent to annex territory, continuing the support pattern established in Syria.

## Background

With Syria annexed and Jordan secured, the LETF identifies Iraq as the keystone of its eastern expansion strategy. Control of Iraq would give the LETF a land border with Turkey to the north, access to the Persian Gulf, and the oil infrastructure necessary to fuel an ever-expanding military. The U.S. backs the operation as part of its ongoing GLA commitments.

[[Magnus Maximus]] commands overall operations with [[Alexander the Great (Fictional LETF Role)]] contributing tactical planning.

## Plan and Execution

### Phase 1: Airstrikes and Missile Bombardments

- **Baghdad** — LETF Su-24 bombers and U.S. B-52 Stratofortresses target key government and military installations
- **Basra** — Southern city targeted to cut off Iraq's oil exports and weaken its economy
- **Mosul** — U.S. airstrikes neutralize northern resistance forces and air defense systems

### Phase 2: Ground Invasion

**Southern Push (LETF from Jordan):**
LETF mechanized units cross into Iraq from the Jordanian Province of the Fade, capturing **Basra** and advancing northwest toward **Nasiriyah**.

**Northern Push (Joint LETF-U.S. from Syria):**
Combined forces advance from Syria, targeting **Mosul** and cutting off northern Iraqi forces from reinforcement.

**Central Push (Joint Forces):**
The main force advances on **Baghdad** from multiple directions, encircling the capital and engaging in heavy urban combat.

### Phase 3: Naval Operations

U.S. and LETF naval forces establish a blockade of the Persian Gulf, preventing Iraqi access to resupply or reinforcement by sea.

## Outcome

| **Territory** | **Outcome** |
|---|---|
| Baghdad | LETF administrative hub; capital of Mesopotamian Province of the Fade |
| Basra | Captured early; critical Persian Gulf port for LETF operations |
| Mosul | Taken after U.S. airstrikes devastate defenses; northern anchor of occupation |

Iraq is declared the **Mesopotamian Province of the Fade**. The U.S. provides overwhelming air and naval support but transfers all territory to LETF administration. Casualties are moderate for LETF forces, with heavy losses for Iraq's military and resistance groups.

## Global Reactions

- **United Nations**: Condemns the annexation of Iraq and plans for Turkey; no effective action.
- **NATO**: Critical tensions — the LETF now borders Turkey, a NATO member, from the south and east.
- **USSR**: Heightened mobilization; Soviet southern border forces are repositioned in response to the LETF reaching the Turkish frontier.
- **Iran**: Deeply alarmed by the LETF's presence along its western border (Iraq) and its northern border (Turkey, via subsequent annexation).

## Legacy

Operation Mesopotamian Fade completes the LETF's Iraq-through-Turkey axis. The campaign's success — accomplished by pivoting directly from Syria to Iraq without consolidation — demonstrates the LETF's logistical capacity but also marks an escalation in supply-chain strain that contributes to later overextension. Iraq's oil infrastructure becomes a key resource base supporting LETF expansion into the Caucasus and Central Asia.
