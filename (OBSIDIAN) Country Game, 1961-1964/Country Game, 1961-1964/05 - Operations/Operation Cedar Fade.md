**Operation Cedar Fade** is the [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]]'s invasion and annexation of Lebanon in 1961, conducted as part of the LETF's systematic expansion through the Levant. The operation is launched shortly after [[Operation Sacred Fade]] secures Israel, and Lebanon is identified both as a strategically valuable coastal state and as a necessary corridor for the planned campaign into Syria ([[Operation Crescent Fade]]). The swift conquest — three phases spanning airstrikes, a ground push, and amphibious coastal operations — takes weeks rather than months, setting a pace that the LETF uses as a template for subsequent Levant campaigns.

## Background

With Israel absorbed and Jordan next on the planning table, the LETF's strategic priority is control over the entire eastern Mediterranean coastline. Lebanon sits directly north of Israel, offering deep-water ports, coastal access, and a land border with Syria. [[Magnus Maximus]] approves the campaign with [[Alexander the Great (Fictional LETF Role)]] contributing tactical planning, drawing on Alexander's historical experience in the Levant.

## Plan and Execution

### Phase 1: Airstrikes

LETF Su-24 bombers conduct sustained strikes on **Beirut** — targeting military installations, government buildings, and infrastructure — and on key air force bases across the country. Within days, Lebanon's air force is effectively neutralized.

### Phase 2: Ground Invasion

**Southern Push:**
LETF mechanized divisions cross into southern Lebanon from the Israeli border (Negev Province of the Fade), rapidly securing:
- **Tyre** — taken with minimal resistance after the surrounding area is softened by air bombardment
- **Sidon** — captured as LETF forces push up the coastal road toward Beirut

**Central Push:**
Forces advance on **Beirut** from the south, engaging in intense urban combat. Street battles last several days before Lebanese military forces collapse and the capital falls.

### Phase 3: Naval Operations

LETF amphibious units land along the northern coast, securing **Tripoli** — Lebanon's second-largest city and key northern port — and completing the encirclement of remaining Lebanese resistance forces. With both the coast and the interior occupied, organized resistance ends.

## Outcome

| **Territory** | **Outcome** |
|---|---|
| Beirut | LETF administrative capital of Lebanon |
| Tripoli | Northern port secured; logistical hub |
| Tyre and Sidon | Southern coastal cities absorbed into LETF occupation |

Lebanon is declared the **Levantine Province of the Fade**. The operation concludes faster than anticipated, freeing LETF forces to reposition for the Syrian campaign. Beirut is established as the regional administrative center and a forward base for [[Operation Crescent Fade]].

## Global Reactions

- **United Nations**: Condemns the annexation but takes no meaningful action.
- **USSR**: The USSR's concern deepens as the LETF secures Mediterranean coastline; Soviet planners begin assessing worst-case scenarios for LETF northern expansion.
- **Arab states**: Alarm spreads among neighboring governments; Saudi Arabia and Jordan internally discuss defensive mobilization.

## Legacy

Operation Cedar Fade demonstrates the LETF's ability to execute rapid sequential conquests — Israel, then Lebanon, then Syria — without pausing to consolidate. This tempo gives opponents no time to coordinate a response and becomes a defining strategic feature of LETF expansion in 1961. The operation also establishes the Levantine Province as a key LETF Mediterranean asset, providing naval access and a springboard for the broader Middle Eastern empire the LETF builds over the following months.
