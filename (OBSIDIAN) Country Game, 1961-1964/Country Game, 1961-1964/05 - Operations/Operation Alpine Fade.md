**Operation Alpine Fade** is the [[Lowest Egyptian Taper Fade (LETF)]]'s 1963 invasion of Switzerland, justified in part by fabricated evidence planted during [[Operation Shadow Recall]] that framed Switzerland as a collaborator in [[Operation Silent Fade]]. The operation is notable for its massive scale relative to Switzerland's size and for the LETF's use of a tactical nuclear weapon against the outskirts of Bern to force the city's surrender. Switzerland's annexation ends the last major European neutral zone and is one of the LETF's final expansions before the 1963 rollback begins.

## Background

Switzerland's neutrality and its position as a refuge for resistance leaders and Silent Fade defectors made it a target. [[Operation Shadow Recall]] provided the diplomatic cover — planted evidence implicated Swiss actors in the assassination of LETF leaders — allowing the LETF to frame the invasion as retaliation rather than unprovoked aggression. The new LETF leadership under [[Operation Eternal Fade]] approves the campaign as one of its first major military actions.

## Campaign Details

### Aerial Assault

The LETF deploys 45 aircraft including Su-24 bombers and MiG-21 fighters:
- **Zurich** — Military installations and government buildings targeted
- **Geneva** — Heavy aerial bombardment; falls quickly
- **Bern** — Military and government targets struck; city's air defenses neutralized

Tear gas and crowd-control agents are deployed in urban centers to incapacitate civilian resistance.

### Missile Barrage

Sustained missile bombardments target military installations, transportation hubs, and Alpine mountain passes. Sustained bombardments isolate resistance forces in the mountain interior.

### Ground Invasion

LETF soldiers equipped with advanced weaponry push into Swiss territory from all borders simultaneously:
- Forces from France ([[Treaty of Paris]] zone) attack from the west
- Forces from Austria and LETF-controlled Germany attack from the east
- Forces from Italy (LETF-controlled) attack from the south

### Nuclear Deployment

One tactical nuclear weapon is detonated on the outskirts of **Bern** — used as a final coercive measure after conventional bombardment fails to force immediate surrender. Bern capitulates; nuclear fallout creates long-term environmental and health challenges in the central Swiss region.

## Outcome

| **Territory** | **Outcome** |
|---|---|
| Zurich | Captured after intense urban combat; LETF administrative capital |
| Geneva | Falls quickly under aerial bombardment |
| Bern | Surrenders following nearby nuclear detonation |
| Alpine regions | LETF holds key passes; guerrilla resistance remains active in remote mountain zones |

Switzerland is declared the **Alpine Fade Province**. The LETF immediately begins implementing Morganist doctrines, replacing Swiss institutional neutrality with LETF militarism and propaganda. However, the Alpine geography makes total control impossible — Swiss resistance fighters continue operating from remote mountain positions.

## Global Reactions

- **United Nations**: Condemns the campaign as "the most aggressive violation of sovereignty in history"; calls for immediate sanctions; no enforcement possible
- **Austria and Sweden**: Escalate covert support for resistance movements within LETF territories, including Switzerland; the Alpine Fade is one of the final escalations before their complete break with the LETF
- **Boombayah**: The framing of Switzerland in Shadow Recall — and the subsequent invasion — convinces Boombayah to expand its sanctuary programs and deepen its coordination with GLA resistance networks

## Legacy

Operation Alpine Fade is one of the LETF's last successful territorial conquests. The empire has now absorbed Switzerland, completing its European control, but the overextension crisis is fully in effect. Within months, the GLA counter-alliance executes [[Operation Silent Fade]], the LETF's leadership is eliminated, and [[Operation Iron Dawn]] begins rolling back former Soviet territories. Switzerland's Alpine resistance networks, sustained by difficult terrain, remain active and contribute to the broader rollback pressure the LETF faces through 1963.
